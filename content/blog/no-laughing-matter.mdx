Every word spoken in Germany's parliament is recorded — and so is every laugh. The Bundestag's stenographic service records not only the spoken word but also the reactions in the plenary chamber.

I made use of these annotations in my research paper and built a macOS program in Swift that downloads, parses, analyzes, and visualizes the protocols. The full paper is available [here](/blog/no-laughing-matter/Research%20Paper.pdf) and the source code can be found on [GitHub](https://github.com/lflorian/no-laughing-matter).


### Less amusement and more laughter

The stenographic service distinguishes between two types of humor markers in the protocols: *Heiterkeit* and *Lachen*. The former denotes laughing *with* someone, with positive intent. *Lachen*, by contrast, is comparable to laughing *at* someone — a more dismissive kind.

In the 18th legislative period (2013–2017), 73% of all humor markers were *Heiterkeit*. By the 21st period (from 2025), that share has dropped to 44%, meaning that the more confrontational *Lachen* now dominates.

![Share of Heiterkeit and Lachen by legislative period](/blog/no-laughing-matter/Heiterkeit-Chart.png)

The sharpest shift occurred between the 18th and 19th periods, coinciding with the entry of the Alternative for Germany (AfD) into parliament in 2017. The total number of *Heiterkeit* and *Lachen* events combined roughly tripled, with nearly all of the growth attributable to additional *Lachen*.

![Humor markers over time — the vertical line marks the AfD's entry into parliament](/blog/no-laughing-matter/Heiterkeit-timeline.png)

The described shift affects all parties, though some show a proportionally higher share of *Lachen* than others.

![Heiterkeit and Lachen by faction](/blog/no-laughing-matter/Heiterkeit-Fractions.png)


### Aggressive humor's share has doubled

To answer not just the question of *when* and *where* the Bundestag laughs, but also *why*, I used an LLM to classify each detected event according to Avner Ziv's five-function model of humor. This model distinguishes between aggressive, social, defensive, intellectual, and sexual humor.

While the shares in the 18th legislative period were still relatively balanced, the 21st period is dominated by an aggressive humor share of 68%.

![Intention distribution by legislative period](/blog/no-laughing-matter/Intention-Distribution.png)


### Who triggers, who reacts

Some actor-level patterns stand out. Normalized by faction size, the Greens and the AfD share the distinction of having the highest humor participation per member — both on the triggering and the reacting side.

Among individual members, Helge Lindh (SPD, 233 events) and Stephan Brandner (AfD, 232 events) stand out, having triggered by far the most humor markers in the protocols. They are followed by Olaf Scholz (SPD, 160 events). Looking at who is recorded as laughing (*Heiterkeit* or *Lachen*) in the protocols, 6 of the top 10 are AfD members — suggesting a particularly demonstrative, individually identifiable laughing behavior.

### What the people involved say

As part of my research paper, I had the opportunity to conduct an interview with Prof. Dr. Norbert Lammert (CDU), who served as President of the German Bundestag from 2005 to 2017. Dr. Gregor Gysi (Die Linke) and the right-wing extremist AfD politician Stephan Brandner also responded to my inquiries.

Prof. Lammert was unsurprised by the shift from *Heiterkeit* to *Lachen*, noting that between the established factions and the AfD there exists a mutual need for demarcation that cannot be bridged through humor. He drew a parallel to the entry of the Greens in 1983, which similarly disrupted parliamentary culture — though more through provocative actions than through targeted polemic.

Dr. Gysi pointed to a broader societal trend toward what he described as an "outrage culture," which is also reflected in parliamentary debates. The AfD in particular, he argued, brings a sharpness that makes humorous exchange difficult. He emphasized self-irony as his preferred rhetorical tool: humor at one's own expense can be deployed without limits, whereas humor at others' expense finds clear boundaries in human dignity.

Brandner described humor as a deliberate rhetorical instrument to expose contradictions and generate reach, portraying the AfD as a target of attacks. The data paints a more nuanced picture: in the 21st legislative period, the AfD produces nearly half of all humor events despite being only the second-largest faction, and has the lowest *Heiterkeit* share of any faction — meaning they laugh (*Lachen*) at others more frequently than any other faction.


### The bigger picture

In our interview, Prof. Lammert observed that what applies to parliament and parliamentary debates generally applies to real life as well. If this is true, the observed shift says something not only about the Bundestag but also about the way people in our society interact with one another. Prof. Lammert recommends presenting oneself with composure and poise rather than fierce determination — for as a rule, it is not those with the greatest zeal who persuade, but those with the greatest composure. Whether this composure will regain more space in the Bundestag in the near future remains to be seen. The data suggests that the path there is currently growing longer rather than shorter.
